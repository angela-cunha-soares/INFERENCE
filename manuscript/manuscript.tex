\begin{abstract}
\textbf{Objective.} Operational irrigation scheduling in tropical
agricultural frontiers demands quantitative uncertainty in
soil-water content, not single-valued deterministic predictions. The
objective of this work is to deliver an open-source, reproducible
Bayesian water-balance framework that (i)~reproduces the FAO-56
daily water balance with calibrated probabilistic uncertainty,
(ii)~issues strictly causal cycle-level and 5-day rolling forecasts
of soybean irrigation depth, and (iii)~quantifies its own skill
against transparent baselines on the Brazilian MATOPIBA agricultural
frontier.

\textbf{Methods.} We present \emph{bwb} (\emph{Bayesian Water
Balance}), a Python package that couples the FAO-56 daily water
balance with a probabilistic inference engine implemented in PyMC
v5. The framework provides three complementary validation modes:
(a) a posterior-recovery test on 150 city $\times$ soil-depth
$\times$ cycle combinations that verifies internal consistency
between the Bayesian model and a deterministic FAO-56 baseline
driven by Xavier reanalysis; (b) a climatological sequential
forecast on 50 city $\times$ cycle combinations that trains
classification priors on 1961--2019, forecasts each cycle by
Monte-Carlo resampling of historical seasons weighted by a
conjugate Dirichlet--Multinomial posterior on a Standardised
Precipitation--Evapotranspiration Index (SPEI) initialised with a
climatologically-informed prior, and updates the prior sequentially
after each observed cycle; and (c) an operational rolling 5-day
forecast on 4{,}250 forecasts that exposes the operational mode of
the pipeline.

\textbf{Results.} Across the ten cropping hubs of MATOPIBA
(Maranh\~ao, Tocantins, Piau\'i, Bahia) and five soybean growing
seasons (2020/21--2024/25), the posterior-recovery test achieved a
median Kling--Gupta Efficiency (KGE) of 0.918 (range 0.798--0.985)
and a 90\% credible-interval coverage of 0.922 against the nominal
0.90, with the worst-case Gelman--Rubin statistic
$\hat{R}\leq 1.005$ across all parameters and zero divergent
transitions. The climatological sequential forecast, evaluated
against the realised seasonal irrigation depth $I_{\mathrm{total}}$
(typically 0--200~mm per cycle in MATOPIBA), achieved a median
Continuous Ranked Probability Score (CRPS) of 18.2~mm\,---\,a
probabilistic mean absolute error of $\approx 10$--$25\%$ of the
typical seasonal depth, with a positive continuous-ranked-probability
skill score (CRPSS $= +0.034$) against a naive long-term-mean
baseline\,---\,and a daily soil-water-content coverage of 0.954
against the nominal 0.90, indicating well-calibrated forecast
intervals. The operational rolling forecast achieved a median KGE of
$+0.32$ on the 5-day cumulative irrigation against the FAO-56
deterministic baseline, with a mean CRPS of 2.65~mm and a 90\%
prediction-interval coverage of 0.970. A global Sobol' sensitivity
analysis identified soil saturation $\theta_s$ (first-order index
0.73) and initial soil moisture $\theta_{\mathrm{init}}$ (0.46) as
the dominant parameters and motivated the prior tuning that lifted
the empirical 90\% coverage from 0.19 (overconfident initial
specification) to 0.92 (calibrated).

\textbf{Conclusion.} The framework satisfies its three stated
objectives: it reproduces the FAO-56 process with calibrated
uncertainty, issues strictly causal probabilistic forecasts that
beat the naive baseline, and exposes a deployable rolling-forecast
mode. It is released under the MIT license with a command-line
interface, Docker container, 97 unit and integration tests, and
continuous integration; the architecture is crop- and
region-agnostic and applies to any FAO-56 crop with daily $P$ and
ETo time series.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
% =====================================================================

Sustainable irrigation in tropical agricultural frontiers requires tools
that quantify uncertainty rather than report point estimates.
The MATOPIBA region\,---\,encompassing portions of Maranh\~ao, Tocantins,
Piau\'i and Bahia in Brazil\,---\,illustrates the tension acutely:
soybean cultivation in pioneering municipalities expanded by more than
twentyfold since the early 1990s \cite{Pousa2019,Santos2023}, while the
local climate exhibits high interannual variability driven by the
Pacific El Ni\~no--Southern Oscillation (ENSO) and the Atlantic
meridional gradient \cite{Grimm2003,Marengo2018}. Recent
system-dynamics analyses project that the rapid expansion of
irrigated land in MATOPIBA may cause $30$--$40\%$ of the local
irrigation demand to remain unmet between 2025 and 2040 under
business-as-usual trajectories \citep{Martins2024MATOPIBA}, which
amplifies the operational value of risk-aware irrigation decisions. A producer who must
decide whether to irrigate today does not need a single predicted value
of soil moisture; she needs the probability that soil-water content
will fall below a critical threshold in the next several days, given
all available information.

The FAO-56 single-crop-coefficient framework \cite{Allen1998}, together
with its companion FAO-66 yield-response formulation
\cite{Steduto2012}, remains the international standard for crop
evapotranspiration and water-deficit accounting. AquaCrop, DSSAT and
similar deterministic models translate FAO-56 into operational decision
support but produce single-valued estimates that ignore observational
uncertainty in the meteorological forcings \cite{Liu2007,Vrugt2016}.
Bayesian methods address this gap by representing system states as
probability distributions and propagating uncertainty from inputs
through to actionable outputs \cite{Gelman2013}. The DREAM family of
algorithms \cite{Vrugt2009,Vrugt2016} demonstrated the operational
viability of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling for hydrological
calibration, and the No-U-Turn Sampler \cite{Hoffman2014} as
implemented in PyMC \cite{AbrilPla2023} now makes posterior inference
tractable on commodity hardware. Recent applications include the work
of \cite{Ribeiro2023}, who used Bayesian regression and Bayesian
networks to estimate ETo at a single Brazilian station, achieving a
root-mean-square error of $0.087$~mm~day$^{-1}$ relative to FAO-56.

Two methodological gaps persist in operational decision support.
First, most published Bayesian water-balance applications calibrate
process parameters at a single site and do not provide a software
artefact that practitioners can deploy regionally without expert
re-implementation. Second, climatological priors derived from
multi-decadal records are rarely combined with sequential learning that
updates the predictive distribution as each cropping season is observed.
The framework presented here addresses both gaps. It (i)~provides a
fully reproducible open-source software framework with a command-line
interface, a Docker container and a continuous-integration pipeline,
applicable to any of the 92 crops parameterised in FAO-56 Table~12
through editing of a single configuration file; (ii)~couples the FAO-56
daily water balance with a Bayesian state-space model in PyMC v5
yielding probabilistic daily soil-water content with formal uncertainty
quantification; (iii)~trains classification priors on the WMO 1961--2019
window and produces a climatological forecast for each subsequent cycle
through Monte-Carlo resampling of historical seasons weighted by a
conjugate Dirichlet--Multinomial posterior on SPEI tercile classes,
sequentially updated as each cycle is observed; and (iv)~validates the
framework across ten MATOPIBA hubs and five soybean cycles
(2020/21--2024/25), reporting both deterministic and probabilistic
skill metrics together with a global Sobol' sensitivity analysis.

We make our software, validation pipeline and figure-generation
scripts publicly available at \url{https://github.com/angela-cunha-soares/INFERENCE}
under the MIT license, archived in Zenodo with a persistent digital
object identifier (DOI:~[XXXX]).

\subsection{Daily climate forcings}\label{sec:methods_data}

Daily meteorological forcings for the 1961--2025 backtest reported in
Section~\ref{sec:results} were obtained from the Brazilian Daily
Weather Gridded Data product \cite{Xavier2022} at $0.25^{\circ}$
resolution, which is to date the only source providing continuous
high-quality precipitation and ETo records for MATOPIBA over the
required 65-year horizon. Two variables enter the water-balance
recursion: precipitation $P$~(mm) and reference evapotranspiration
ETo~(mm) computed by the FAO-Penman--Monteith equation
\cite{Allen1998}. The five auxiliary variables (maximum and minimum
temperature, relative humidity, incoming solar radiation, 2-m wind
speed) are used in the climatological characterisation of the
training period (Section~\ref{sec:methods_priors}) but do not enter
the Bayesian likelihood directly. The dataset provides
$23{,}741$~daily records per hub.

\paragraph{Operational forcings (2026 onwards).}
Because the curated Xavier reanalysis is not updated in real time and
cannot be queried from a farm-management software, the operational
deployment of the rolling forecast (Section~\ref{sec:methods_forecast})
substitutes a multi-source fused product. Three open-data sources are
combined: NASA POWER (MERRA-2, 1990--present, daily, global)
\cite{NASAPower2024}, Open-Meteo Archive (ERA5, 1940--present, daily,
global) \cite{OpenMeteo2024}, and Open-Meteo Forecast (best-match NWP,
today\,$\pm$\,5\,d) for the operational horizon only. For each variable,
NASA POWER and Open-Meteo Archive are combined through a per-variable
weighted average with weights $w_\mathrm{NASA} = 0.92$ for shortwave
radiation, $0.20$ for wind at 2\,m, $0.35$ for relative humidity,
$0.58$ for $T_\mathrm{max}$, $0.52$ for $T_\mathrm{min}$ and $0.50$
for precipitation; weights are calibrated by \citet{Ribeiro2024}
against BR-DWGD at 17 Brazilian sites and shown to transfer to
MATOPIBA without retuning in Appendix~\ref{app:inmet_validation}.
Wind at 10\,m from Open-Meteo is converted to the FAO-56 reference 2\,m
height through Eq.\,47 of \citet{Allen1998}, $u_2 = u_{10}\cdot
4.87/\ln(67.8\cdot 10 - 5.42)$. Reference evapotranspiration of the
fused product is recomputed by Penman-Monteith on the fused inputs
rather than averaged from the per-source ETo, following
\citet{Ribeiro2024}.

A 127-day side-by-side validation against in situ INMET automatic
stations (Appendix~\ref{app:inmet_validation}) shows that the fused
product reproduces the daily observations more faithfully than either
individual source, with pooled RMSEs of $0.54$\,mm\,d$^{-1}$ for ETo
and $7.92$\,mm\,d$^{-1}$ for daily precipitation
(Table~\ref{tab:inmet-validation}), and improves over two
reduced-input alternatives commonly recommended for data-sparse
regions, Hargreaves-Samani \cite{Hargreaves1985} and Benavides-Lopez
\cite{Benavides1970}, by a factor of two to three on the same Xavier
window (Table~\ref{tab:eto-alternatives};
Figure~\ref{fig:eto_alternatives}). For this reason, the
operational pipeline keeps the full Penman-Monteith equation
recomputed on the fused inputs rather than substituting a reduced
formula. The 1961--2025 backtest reported in
Section~\ref{sec:results} is performed on Xavier alone --- because no
operational reanalysis covers the pre-1990 period --- but the same
forecast machinery accepts a fused or NWP ensemble for production
use.

\subsection{Climatological characterisation and prior elicitation}
\label{sec:methods_priors}

We computed climatological normals over three World Meteorological
Organization standard reference periods: 1961--1990, 1981--2010 and
1991--2020 \cite{WMO2017}. Differences between periods were assessed
with the Kruskal--Wallis $H$ test followed by Bonferroni-corrected
pairwise Mann--Whitney $U$ tests on every city $\times$ variable
$\times$ month combination, documenting the magnitude of inter-period
non-stationarity at each hub.

For each city $\times$ variable $\times$ month combination, the
empirical distributional family was identified through a four-step
information-theoretic protocol \cite{Burnham2002}. Candidate families
were drawn from theoretically appropriate sets (Zero-Inflated Gamma for
daily precipitation; Gamma or Log-Normal for ETo; Skew-Normal for
temperatures; Beta on rescaled $[0,1]$ for relative humidity; Weibull
for wind speed). Maximum-likelihood parameters were estimated for every
candidate, and the candidate minimising the Akaike Information Criterion
\cite{Akaike1974} (with $\Delta\mathrm{AIC} < 2$ as evidence of
indistinguishability and the more parsimonious model preferred) was
retained. Goodness-of-fit was confirmed through Kolmogorov--Smirnov and
Anderson--Darling tests at $\alpha = 0.05$. The resulting distribution
atlas (city $\times$ variable $\times$ month $\to$ family $+$
parameters) is consumed by the climatological forecast pipeline
(Section~\ref{sec:methods_forecast}) for prior elicitation of seasonal
indices.

\subsection{FAO-56 daily water balance}\label{sec:methods_fao56}

The deterministic water-balance recursion follows FAO-56 Chapter~6
\cite{Allen1998}. Let $\mathrm{SW}_d$ denote the soil-water content
(mm) on day $d$ over an effective root depth $Z_r = 60$~cm of a
soybean cultivar with available water capacity $\mathrm{AWC} = 120$~mm
and management-allowed depletion $\mathrm{MAD} = 0.55$. Effective
precipitation is taken as $P_{\mathrm{eff},d} = 0.8 \cdot P_d$
(FAO-56 simplified runoff). Crop evapotranspiration is
$\mathrm{ET}_{c,d} = K_{c,d} \cdot \mathrm{ET}_{0,d}$ with the
piecewise-linear $K_c$ curve of Table~\ref{tab:kc}. The daily update
is
%
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{SW}_d &= \mathrm{SW}_{d-1} + P_{\mathrm{eff},d} - \mathrm{ET}_{c,d}, \\
\mathrm{DP}_d &= \max(0, \mathrm{SW}_d - \mathrm{AWC}), \\
\mathrm{SW}_d &= \min(\mathrm{SW}_d, \mathrm{AWC}),
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:wb_daily}
\end{equation}
%
where $\mathrm{DP}_d$ is deep percolation; an illustrative
trajectory of the resulting soil-water content over a 90-day cycle
is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:water_balance_timeseries}. The
irrigation decision rule is triggered whenever $\mathrm{SW}_d$
falls below the threshold $\mathrm{AWC} \cdot (1-\mathrm{MAD}) =
54$~mm:
%
\begin{equation}
I_d =
\begin{cases}
\mathrm{AWC} - \mathrm{SW}_d, & \text{if } \mathrm{SW}_d < \mathrm{AWC}(1-\mathrm{MAD}), \\
0, & \text{otherwise},
\end{cases}
\quad \mathrm{SW}_d \leftarrow \mathrm{SW}_d + I_d.
\label{eq:irrig_rule}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Bayesian inference architecture}\label{sec:methods_inference}

The full pipeline that connects the daily forcings of
Section~\ref{sec:methods_data} to the operational forecast outputs is
summarised in Figure~\ref{fig:flowchart}. The framework combines two complementary modes of Bayesian inference,
each chosen for the analytical structure of its posterior. Both are
exact Bayesian inference; the choice between them is governed by
efficiency, not by epistemological commitment.

\paragraph{Conjugate inference for yearly class weights.}
The Dirichlet--Multinomial pair is conjugate
\cite{Gelman2013}: given counts $\mathbf{n}=(n_d,n_n,n_w)$ of dry,
normal and wet historical seasons in the training window, the
posterior on the season-class probability vector $\mathbf{w}$ is
$\mathbf{w}\mid\mathbf{n} \sim \mathrm{Dir}(\boldsymbol{\alpha}_0+\mathbf{n})$
in closed form. Sequential cycle-by-cycle updates follow
$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{c+1}=\boldsymbol{\alpha}_c+\mathbf{e}_{\kappa^*_c}$
without numerical approximation. This component therefore does not
require Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC); applying MCMC here would
produce identical posteriors at greater computational cost and with
an avoidable Monte-Carlo error term \cite[Ch.\,3]{Gelman2013}.

\paragraph{NUTS for the non-conjugate hydraulic state space.}
The hydraulic and crop parameters $\theta_s,\theta_r,K_{c,\mathrm{mult}},
\theta_\mathrm{init},\sigma_\mathrm{obs}$ enter the FAO-56 recursion
nonlinearly through the clip operator and the daily integration.
The posterior in this layer has no closed form, and we therefore
sample it with the No-U-Turn Sampler \cite{Hoffman2014}, the
Hamiltonian-Monte-Carlo variant currently considered the most
efficient gradient-based sampler for moderate-dimensional latent-
state models \cite{Betancourt2017}. The implementation uses PyMC v5
\cite{AbrilPla2023}; a separate posterior-recovery test
(Section~\ref{sec:methods_pymc}) verifies that the sampler reproduces
known parameter values when the data are generated from the model
itself.

\paragraph{Posterior predictive checks and DAG structure learning.}
Two diagnostic outputs accompany the inference machinery:
ArviZ \cite{Kumar2019} posterior predictive checks against the
deterministic FAO-56 baseline (Section~\ref{sec:results_calibration}),
and a Bayesian network learnt over the observed climatic variables
through the BDeu-scored K2 algorithm
(Section~\ref{sec:methods_dag}, Appendix~\ref{app:dag}), used to
identify the variables most strongly conditioning ETo and to
contextualise the irrelevance of precipitation in the
Penman--Monteith decomposition.

\subsection{Bayesian state-space model and posterior-recovery test}
\label{sec:methods_pymc}

The Bayesian state-space model in PyMC v5 \cite{AbrilPla2023} treats
the four hydraulic and crop parameters as random variables and
recovers their posterior distribution given a reference soil-water
trajectory. Priors are truncated normals (TN) informed by the
regional pedological reference (Tables~\ref{tab:soils-matopiba} and
\ref{tab:van-genuchten-matopiba}) and by the FAO-56 crop library:
%
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\theta_s &\sim \mathrm{TN}(\mu_{\theta_s}, \sigma_{\theta_s}=0.04;\ 0.20, 0.60), \\
\theta_r &\sim \mathrm{TN}(\mu_{\theta_r}, \sigma_{\theta_r}=0.02;\ 0.00, 0.20), \\
K_{c,\mathrm{mult}} &\sim \mathrm{TN}(1.0, 0.08;\ 0.70, 1.30), \\
\theta_{\mathrm{init}} &\sim \mathrm{TN}\bigl(\tfrac{\theta_s+\theta_r}{2}, 0.06;\ \theta_r, \theta_s\bigr).
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:priors}
\end{equation}
%
We comment on each prior and on \emph{why} the choice matters for
the posterior. The saturated water content $\theta_s$ is the
volumetric soil-moisture upper bound; its prior centre
$\mu_{\theta_s}$ comes from the van Genuchten retention parameters of
the regional reference profile (Table~\ref{tab:van-genuchten-matopiba})
and the truncation $[0.20, 0.60]$ excludes physically implausible
values (the lower bound is below the residual moisture of any
realistic textural class for MATOPIBA, the upper bound is above the
saturation of any pedotransfer-derived sandy or sandy-loam profile).
A wider $\sigma_{\theta_s}$ would let $\theta_s$ wander outside the
operational range and inflate the posterior on the dominant
parameter, with measurable damage to coverage; a narrower one would
collapse the posterior onto the prior centre and prevent the data
from refining it. The Sobol' analysis
(Section~\ref{sec:results_sensitivity}) identifies $\theta_s$ as the
single most important parameter for the seasonal cumulative
depletion, with first-order Sobol' index $S_1=0.73$, which makes the
choice of $\sigma_{\theta_s}$ a load-bearing modelling decision.
The residual water content $\theta_r$ has a tight Sobol' index
($S_T \approx 0$) over wet soybean cycles, so a tighter
$\sigma_{\theta_r}=0.02$ is appropriate: posterior identifiability
of $\theta_r$ from these data is low, and a wider prior would only
add sampler workload without improving the predictive distribution.
The crop-coefficient multiplier $K_{c,\mathrm{mult}}$ is centred at
$1.0$ (no scaling of the FAO-56 reference $K_c$ curve of
Table~\ref{tab:kc}) with a $\pm 30\%$ truncation that admits the
documented genotype $\times$ environment variability of
\textit{Glycine max} reported in Brazilian agronomy
\citep{EmbrapaSoja}. The initial soil moisture
$\theta_{\mathrm{init}}$ is centred at $(\theta_s+\theta_r)/2$ with
$\sigma_{\theta_{\mathrm{init}}}=0.06$ and is hierarchically tied to
$\theta_s$ and $\theta_r$ through the truncation bounds: this lets
the prior \emph{adapt} as $\theta_s$ and $\theta_r$ are refined,
which is how a small but informative observation window can lift
coverage by $0.30$ in absolute terms over a non-hierarchical
specification (Table~\ref{tab:calibration_history}). The hydraulic
state then evolves through a clipped FAO-56 recursion in volumetric
soil water $\theta_d$ (m$^3$~m$^{-3}$), with the same forcings $P,
\mathrm{ET}_0$ as Eq.~\ref{eq:wb_daily}, and the observation
likelihood is
%
\begin{equation}
\theta_{\mathrm{obs},d} \sim \mathcal{N}(\theta_d, \sigma_{\mathrm{obs}}),
\quad \sigma_{\mathrm{obs}} \sim \mathrm{HalfNormal}(0.10).
\label{eq:obs_likelihood}
\end{equation}

The observation-noise scale $\sigma_{\mathrm{obs}}$ has a HalfNormal
prior with mode at zero and a long right tail truncated at the
empirically calibrated value $\sigma_{\mathrm{obs}}^{\max}=0.10$.
This is the prior \emph{whose width directly controls the coverage}
of the 90\% credible interval: too narrow a prior collapses the
posterior predictive onto the latent $\theta$ trajectory and
yields under-coverage ($0.19$ in the v0 calibration of
Table~\ref{tab:calibration_history}); the v2 specification reported
above raised coverage to the calibrated $0.92$ and is the prior we
recommend for any future deployment of the framework.

The reference trajectory $\theta_{\mathrm{obs},d}$ is the
deterministic FAO-56 baseline of Eq.~\ref{eq:wb_daily} translated to
volumetric units. Posterior inference uses the No-U-Turn Sampler
\cite{Hoffman2014} with 600 warm-up draws and 600 post-warm-up samples
on four parallel chains (2{,}400 post-warm-up draws per cycle).
Sampling diagnostics for all five 2020--2025 cycles are summarised in
Table~\ref{tab:mcmc-diagnostics}: the worst-case Gelman--Rubin
statistic across all parameters and cycles is $\hat{R}=1.005$ (target
$\hat{R}<1.05$), the smallest bulk effective sample size is
$\mathrm{ESS}_\mathrm{bulk}=1{,}028$ (target $\geq 100$ per parameter
\citep{Vehtari2021}), and the sampler reports zero divergent
transitions across the entire 12{,}000-draw run, confirming no
geometric pathology in the posterior.

\input{../output/paper_tables/table_mcmc_diagnostics.tex}

We refer to this exercise as the \emph{posterior-recovery test}: in
the spirit of \cite{Cook2006,Talts2018}, the procedure feeds the
Bayesian model with a synthetic ``observation'' generated by the same
FAO-56 process under fixed parameter values, and verifies that the
posterior distribution recovers parameter values and trajectories
consistent with that synthetic ground truth. A failure here would
indicate a software bug, a poorly specified prior, or sampler
pathology; success is a necessary precondition for any subsequent
forecast claim, but is itself a test of internal self-consistency
rather than of predictive skill against a real observed cycle. Probabilistic metrics are computed on the
posterior predictive distribution of $\theta_{\mathrm{obs}}$ rather
than on the latent $\theta$ posterior, since only the predictive
distribution carries the observation noise that the data express
\cite{Vehtari2017}.

\subsection{DAG structure learning over climate variables}
\label{sec:methods_dag}

To make explicit which climate variables condition ETo and which are
conditionally independent of it, a directed acyclic graph (DAG) is
learnt over the eight FAO-56 inputs (Tmax, Tmin, Tmean, RH, u2, Rs,
P) plus ETo. The methodological precedent is the IJCNN study of
\citet{Ribeiro2022}, who learnt a Bayesian network for ETo over
five INMET stations of MATOPIBA using HillClimb search and the BDeu
score on \verb|pgmpy| \citep{Ankan2015} and identified
$u_2$ and minimum relative humidity as the parents of ETo on a
$\approx 5{,}000$-record daily series; we adopt the same toolchain
to make the comparison apples-to-apples and broaden the input set
(adding $T_\mathrm{mean}$, $T_\mathrm{min}$, $T_\mathrm{max}$,
$R_\mathrm{s}$ and $P$) and the population (the pooled 65-year
Xavier reanalysis across the ten cropping hubs of MATOPIBA, $n
\approx 237{,}410$). We use hill-climbing search
\citep{Russell2010} guided by two information-theoretic scoring
functions, the BDeu prior of \citet{Buntine1991} and the Bayesian
Information Criterion of \citet{Schwarz1978}, and retain only edges
on which the two scorers agree. Continuous variables are discretised
by 5-quantile binning (equal-frequency); the explicit binning
ensures reproducibility, an omission noted in the discrete-BN
literature \citep{Hui2022}. The learner is implemented in
\verb|bwb.inference.dag_learning| on top of \verb|pgmpy|.
The resulting DAG (Figure~\ref{fig:dag-climate} and
Table~\ref{tab:dag-relevance}) recovers $R_\mathrm{s}$,
$T_\mathrm{min}$ and $u_2$ as the direct parents of ETo, with $P$
identified as conditionally independent of ETo --- a structural
recovery of the empirical observation in \citet{Ribeiro2024} and
consistent with the absence of $P$ from the Penman-Monteith
expression itself. The recovered parent set is consistent with
\citet{Ribeiro2022} on the variables they shared with us
($u_2$ recovered as a parent in both works); the inclusion of
$R_\mathrm{s}$ and $T_\mathrm{min}$ as additional parents in our
study is a direct consequence of the larger pooled sample and
broader variable set, both of which give the BDeu/BIC
agreement enough power to retain edges that the smaller per-station
INMET dataset could not. The interpretation is that all three
parents were always there in the Penman--Monteith expression; the
data-driven DAG simply detects the strongest of them.

\subsection{ENSO-conditioned dynamic Bayesian network}
\label{sec:methods_dbn}

The Dirichlet posterior of Section~\ref{sec:methods_priors} treats
yearly class draws as exchangeable. Inter-annual variability,
however, is correlated with the El Niño/Southern Oscillation (ENSO)
\citep{Cai2014}. To encode this dependency without giving up the
modular forecast architecture, we introduce a dynamic Bayesian
network (DBN, \citealt{Murphy2002}) in which season class
$\kappa_t$ depends on the seasonal ENSO regime
$r_t \in \{\mathrm{La\,Ni\tilde{n}a}, \mathrm{neutral},
\mathrm{El\,Ni\tilde{n}o}\}$ classified from the 3-month running
ONI \citep{NOAA_ONI} crossing the standard $\pm 0.5\,^\circ$C
threshold. The model places one Dirichlet prior per regime,
%
\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{\alpha}_0 \sim \mathrm{HalfNormal}(5)^{\otimes 3},
\quad
\boldsymbol{\pi}_r \mid \boldsymbol{\alpha}_0 \sim
   \mathrm{Dir}(\boldsymbol{\alpha}_0),
\quad
\kappa_t \mid r_t \sim \mathrm{Cat}(\boldsymbol{\pi}_{r_t}),
\label{eq:dbn}
\end{equation}
%
i.e.\ a Markov-1 transition kernel parameterised by ENSO. With
$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_0$ random, the posterior is no longer
conjugate; we sample it with NUTS (4 chains, 1{,}000 draws,
target acceptance 0.95). Convergence is excellent for all
nine $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ parameters (max $\hat{R}=1.004$, min
ESS$_\mathrm{bulk}=2{,}127$, zero divergences;
Table~\ref{tab:dbn-enso}). The result (Figure~\ref{fig:dbn-enso})
shows that ENSO conditioning sharpens the dry-class probability in
El Niño seasons from the static $0.34$ to $0.38$ and shifts the
neutral regime towards the wet class, which provides the
methodological route to a forecast that blends climatological
analogues with the seasonal ENSO state in operational mode.

\subsection{Climatological sequential forecast}\label{sec:methods_forecast}

The climatological sequential forecast is the production validation
of the framework. For each target cycle $c$
(planting year $c$, harvest year $c+1$):

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Training set.} All complete soybean cycles in
$1961, \ldots, c-1$ are extracted from the Xavier daily series. Years
2020/21 through 2024/25 use $59, 60, 61, 62, 63$ training cycles
respectively.

\item \textbf{Seasonal SPEI classification.} For each historical cycle
the seasonal water balance $D = P_{\mathrm{total}} - \mathrm{ET}_{0,\mathrm{total}}$
is computed and standardised through the SPEI of Vicente-Serrano et al.
\cite{VicenteSerrano2010}: a log-logistic distribution is fitted by
maximum likelihood on the (shift-corrected) $D$ series, the empirical
CDF is mapped through the inverse standard-normal CDF, and the
resulting SPEI series is partitioned by terciles into the three
classes $\{\text{dry}, \text{normal}, \text{wet}\}$ following the
quantile convention of \cite{Pinkayan1966,Xavier2002}. We retain the
in-house log-logistic SPEI computation rather than a pre-computed
gridded SPEI product because the seasonal water balance must be
specific to the soybean planting calendar of each MATOPIBA hub; the
2024 release of the global multi-timescale daily SPEI dataset
\citep{Wang2024SPEIGD} is an alternative source for monthly tracking
that the framework can ingest through the
\verb|bwb.priors.indices| module without changing the resampling
machinery.

\item \textbf{Conjugate Dirichlet--Multinomial update.} The class
counts are combined with a Dirichlet prior:
%
\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{w} \mid \mathrm{counts} \sim \mathrm{Dirichlet}(\boldsymbol{\alpha}_c), \quad
\boldsymbol{\alpha}_c = \boldsymbol{\alpha}_{c-1} + \boldsymbol{e}_{\mathrm{class}(c-1)},
\label{eq:dirichlet}
\end{equation}
%
where the initial hyperparameter at the first target cycle is the
\emph{climatologically-informed} prior
$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{2020} = \boldsymbol{n}_{1961\text{--}2019} +
\boldsymbol{1}$, with $\boldsymbol{n}_{1961\text{--}2019}$ the
SPEI-tercile counts on the training set (typically
$\boldsymbol{n}\approx(20,20,19)$ across the 59 training cycles by
construction of equal-frequency terciles) and $\boldsymbol{1}$ a
unit smoother per class to keep the prior strictly proper, and
$\boldsymbol{e}_k$ is the one-hot encoding of the class observed
at cycle $c-1$. The framework also exposes the uniform-prior
variant ($\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{2020}=(1,1,1)$) for ablation; on this
validation grid it inflates the median CRPS from 18.2~mm to
$\approx 23$~mm because the five available target cycles are too few
to refine an uninformative posterior. The conjugacy of
Dirichlet--Multinomial yields a closed-form posterior; no MCMC is
required.

\item \textbf{Monte-Carlo forecast.} For each of $N = 500$ simulations
we (i)~sample $\boldsymbol{w} \sim \mathrm{Dir}(\boldsymbol{\alpha}_c)$,
(ii)~sample a class $k \sim \mathrm{Cat}(\boldsymbol{w})$,
(iii)~sample uniformly one historical cycle of class $k$, and
(iv)~run the daily water balance of Eq.~\ref{eq:wb_daily} on the
resampled $(P, \mathrm{ET}_0)$ pair. The aggregate produces the
forecast distribution of the daily soil-water content
$\mathrm{SW}_{c,d}$ and irrigation depth $I_{c,d}$.

\item \textbf{Evaluation against the realised cycle.} The realised
$(P, \mathrm{ET}_0)$ for cycle $c$ from the Xavier reanalysis is fed
through Eq.~\ref{eq:wb_daily} to obtain the observed
$(\mathrm{SW}_{\mathrm{obs}}, I_{\mathrm{obs}})$, and skill metrics
are computed (Section~\ref{sec:methods_metrics}).

\item \textbf{Sequential update.} The realised cycle is classified
using the training-set tercile thresholds (no retraining); its
one-hot class vector increments
$\boldsymbol{\alpha} \to \boldsymbol{\alpha}_{c+1}$, which becomes the
prior of the next cycle.
\end{enumerate}

The information flow is strictly causal: when forecasting cycle $c$
the model uses only data from $1961, \ldots, c-1$. The SPEI
thresholds, the resampled forcings and the Dirichlet $\alpha$ all
satisfy this constraint.

\subsection{Validation metrics}\label{sec:methods_metrics}

We use a calibrated combination of deterministic and probabilistic
skill scores. Each metric was selected for a specific question that
risk-aware irrigation scheduling must answer, and we make the
operational meaning explicit below.

\paragraph{Deterministic accuracy.} The Kling--Gupta Efficiency
\citep{Gupta2009},
$\mathrm{KGE}=1-\sqrt{(r-1)^2+(\sigma_s/\sigma_o-1)^2+(\mu_s/\mu_o-1)^2}$,
decomposes the error into correlation $r$, variability ratio
$\sigma_s/\sigma_o$ and bias ratio $\mu_s/\mu_o$; values $>0.75$
indicate that the median forecast captures more than three quarters
of the variance \emph{and} of the magnitude of the reference
trajectory, while $\mathrm{KGE}\leq 0$ indicates the climatological
mean is at least as good as the forecast on the daily level. The
Nash--Sutcliffe Efficiency \citep{NashSutcliffe1970},
$\mathrm{NSE}=1-\sum (s_d-o_d)^2/\sum(o_d-\bar o)^2$, has the same
upper bound but penalises high-magnitude errors more strongly because
it is squared-error based; $\mathrm{NSE}>0.75$ is conventionally
``very good'' for hydrologic models \citep{Moriasi2007}. RMSE and
MAE quantify the absolute residual between median forecast and
reference in the original units (m$^3$~m$^{-3}$ for soil-water
content, mm for irrigation depth); their ratio measures the
skewness of the error distribution. Bias is the signed mean
residual (negative = under-prediction); the percent bias
\citep{Moriasi2007} normalises it by the observed mean and is
considered satisfactory when $|\mathrm{PBIAS}|\leq 25\%$ for
hydrologic models. The Pearson correlation $r$ enters our suite
implicitly through the first KGE component above and explicitly
through the coefficient of determination $r^2$ when the latter is
required; we do not report $r^2$ as a stand-alone scalar because
the Nash--Sutcliffe efficiency reduces to $r^2$ when the forecast
mean and variance match the observed values, so a separate $r^2$
column would duplicate information already carried by KGE and NSE.
We further note that the framework does not apply any \emph{post
hoc} bias-correction (linear scaling, quantile mapping or delta
change) to the raw FAO-56 trajectory, by design: the posterior
predictive distribution carries a calibrated bias as part of the
uncertainty budget, and an external bias correction would distort
the calibration of the credible intervals (Section~\ref{sec:results_calibration}).
A bias-corrected delivery layer for downstream operational dashboards
is exposed as an opt-in post-processor in
\verb|bwb.decision.utility| and is documented as a deployment
decision rather than as a validation step.

\paragraph{Probabilistic skill.} The Continuous Ranked Probability
Score \citep{Hersbach2000},
$\mathrm{CRPS}(F,o) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} (F(x)-\mathbb{1}\{x\geq o\})^2 \mathrm{d}x$,
is the strictly proper analogue of MAE for predictive distributions
$F$: it equals the MAE of a deterministic forecast when $F$ collapses
to a point mass, and otherwise rewards distributions that are
\emph{both} accurate and sharp. CRPS is reported in the same units
as the variable being forecast and is the operational scalar we use
to rank candidate forecasters. The coverage of the 90\% credible
interval, $\mathrm{cov}_{90}=\mathrm{Pr}(o\in[q_{0.05},q_{0.95}])$,
is the empirical probability that the realised value falls inside
the nominal $90\%$ band; an under-coverage ($<0.85$) indicates
overconfident intervals (operationally dangerous) while a slight
over-coverage ($\sim 0.95$) indicates conservative intervals
(operationally safe). The PIT-based $\alpha$-reliability index of
\citet{Renard2010} measures the area between the empirical PIT
staircase and the diagonal of the unit square: $\alpha=1$ corresponds
to a perfectly calibrated forecaster (PIT distributed uniformly on
$[0,1]$), $\alpha=0$ to a degenerate forecaster. The interval score
of \citet{GneitingRaftery2007},
$\mathrm{IS}_{0.10}=(q_{0.95}-q_{0.05})+\frac{2}{0.10}[(q_{0.05}-o)_++(o-q_{0.95})_+]$,
combines the width of the 90\% interval with the magnitude of any
exceedance, penalising both excessively wide intervals and
exceedances proportionally; it admits a unique minimum at the
oracle forecast and is therefore a strictly proper score
\citep{GneitingRaftery2007}. Finally, for the climatological forecast
we report the continuous-ranked-probability skill score $\mathrm{CRPSS}=
1-\mathrm{CRPS}_{\mathrm{model}}/\mathrm{CRPS}_{\mathrm{baseline}}$
against three baselines (naive long-term-mean climatology,
persistence, and naive sample mean): values $>0$ indicate skill
above the baseline, $=0$ indistinguishability, $<0$ skill below
the baseline. All metrics are implemented in
\texttt{bwb.validation.metrics}, which is exercised by the unit-test
suite at every commit. We deliberately retain the standard suite
above (KGE, NSE, CRPS, coverage, $\alpha$-PIT, interval score,
CRPSS) rather than adopting recent alternatives such as the
information-theoretic RUMI (ratio of uncertainty to mutual
information) of \citet{Pizarro2025RUMI}, because the standard
suite is what the irrigation, hydrological and probabilistic
forecasting communities currently use to benchmark each other; an
RUMI-style information-theoretic objective is a tractable extension
that we expose as an opt-in module in
\verb|bwb.validation.metrics| for future
methodological-comparison studies.

\subsection{Sensitivity analysis}\label{sec:methods_sensitivity}

Parameter importance was assessed through Sobol' global sensitivity
indices \cite{Saltelli2010}. We sampled $1{,}000$ Saltelli-design
points over the four-parameter space $(\theta_s, \theta_r,
K_{c,\mathrm{mult}}, \theta_{\mathrm{init}})$ with bounds taken from
the prior support of Eq.~\ref{eq:priors}, ran the deterministic
water balance for each, and used the seasonal cumulative depletion
$\sum_d \max(0, \theta_s - \theta_d)$ as the scalar output. The
first-order $S_1$ and total-order $S_T$ Sobol' indices were estimated
through the Saltelli (2010) variance-decomposition formulas.

\subsection{Climatological characterisation}\label{sec:results_climate}

The 65-year climatological characterisation showed coherent
inter-period non-stationarity in temperature and reference

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Two complementary validation modes}

The framework provides two distinct validation modes that should not
be conflated. The posterior-recovery test (Section~\ref{sec:results_pymc})
verifies that the Bayesian implementation reproduces the FAO-56
deterministic process within calibrated uncertainty; the
near-perfect KGE ($0.918$ median) and the calibrated coverage
($0.922$) demonstrate that the PyMC code is faithful and the priors
are well-tuned. This is a necessary condition for any subsequent
operational claim, but it is not by itself a forecast skill score.

The climatological sequential forecast
(Section~\ref{sec:results_forecast}) is the operational validation
of interest. Here the model is trained strictly on $1961, \ldots, c-1$
data and forecasts cycle $c$ before observing it. The forecast is
\emph{climatological}: it does not target the realised daily
trajectory but the distribution from which it is drawn. The KGE on
the daily soil-water trajectory is correspondingly negative on
average ($-0.10$), which is the correct behaviour for this kind of
forecast. The calibration of the credible intervals is, by contrast,
very strong: $0.954$ daily soil-water coverage and $0.96$
irrigation-depth coverage at the nominal $0.90$ level, with a mean
PIT of $0.46$ on the seasonal irrigation depth confirming
near-uniformity of the predictive distribution. This means that,
when the framework reports a $90\%$ credible interval for the
seasonal irrigation depth, the realised value falls inside that
interval $\approx 95\%$--$96\%$ of the time across the full
validation grid; in addition, the forecast attains a positive CRPSS
of $+0.034$ against a naive long-term-mean baseline. For operational
risk-aware decision making the calibration of the intervals is the
metric that matters.

\subsection{The role of the priors in the reported skill}
\label{sec:discussion_priors}

The reported skill of the framework is the joint outcome of the
likelihood, the data and the priors. We make the contribution of the
priors explicit because two of them are the dominant levers between
an under-calibrated and a calibrated framework, and because that
contribution is reproducible.

\paragraph{Hydraulic priors close the coverage gap.} The
calibration history (Table~\ref{tab:calibration_history}) traces the
empirical 90\% coverage from $0.19$ in the v0 specification to
$0.92$ in the v2 specification. Two changes drove the gap to close:
(i) widening the observation-noise prior
$\sigma_{\mathrm{obs}}\sim\mathrm{HalfNormal}(0.10)$, which lets the
posterior predictive distribution carry the residual error of the
FAO-56 process rather than artificially collapsing it onto the
latent $\theta$ trajectory; and (ii) relaxing the priors on the
two parameters identified by Sobol' as dominant
($\sigma_{\theta_s}: 0.03\to 0.04$,
$\sigma_{\theta_{\mathrm{init}}}: 0.04\to 0.06$), which lets the
data refine $\theta_s$ and $\theta_{\mathrm{init}}$ rather than
shrinking them to the prior centre. The subsequent posterior
predictive coverage of $0.92$ is statistically indistinguishable
from the nominal $0.90$ level; the same priors deliver a posterior
$\hat{R}<1.005$ across all five cycles
(Table~\ref{tab:mcmc-diagnostics}), confirming that the relaxation
did not introduce sampler pathology.

\paragraph{The climatologically-informed Dirichlet prior closes the
small-sample gap.} For the climatological forecast, the difference
between the uniform-prior ablation
($\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{2020}=(1,1,1)$) and the
climatologically-informed prior
($\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{2020}=\boldsymbol{n}_{1961\text{--}2019}+\boldsymbol{1}$)
is the difference between a median CRPS of $\approx 23$~mm and the
reported $18.2$~mm. Five forecast cycles are not enough to refine an
uninformative Dirichlet posterior into a useful tercile-class
weight; the 59-year training window, by contrast, contributes
$\boldsymbol{n}\approx(20,20,19)$ effective counts that anchor the
posterior predictive distribution at the climatological tercile
frequencies of MATOPIBA. The sequential update preserves the
information from the training window while still letting each
observed cycle update the posterior\,---\,the contribution of the
prior is non-degenerate but bounded.

\paragraph{Conjugate inference is a deliberate efficiency choice,
not an epistemological one.} The Dirichlet--Multinomial layer is
conjugate \citep{Gelman2013}; the posterior of
Eq.~\ref{eq:dirichlet} is exact and closed-form. Applying NUTS to
the same posterior would yield identical credible intervals at
greater cost and with an avoidable Monte-Carlo error term. The
non-conjugate hydraulic state space, on the other hand, has no
closed-form posterior, and we therefore use NUTS \citep{Hoffman2014}
which is currently considered the most efficient gradient-based
sampler for moderate-dimensional latent-state models
\citep{Betancourt2017}. The combination of conjugate and NUTS
inference\,---\,each chosen for the analytical structure of its
posterior\,---\,delivers the operational pipeline (full validation
grid in 8.2~seconds) that the framework offers; it is exact
Bayesian inference end-to-end.

\subsection{Operational interpretation: calibration first, raw skill
second}\label{sec:discussion_calibration_first}

The continuous-ranked-probability skill score of the
climatological forecast against a naive long-term-mean baseline is
modest in absolute terms ($\mathrm{CRPSS}=+0.034$ on average across
the 50 cycle--hub combinations). We discuss explicitly why this
modest absolute value is the correct operational outcome, and why
the calibration of the credible intervals is the metric that should
be prioritised in the agricultural-frontier setting.

\paragraph{The CRPSS baseline is itself uninformative-but-strong.}
Unlike short-range numerical weather prediction, the climatological
mode here uses no information from the target cycle at all: the
training horizon is closed at $c-1$ and the resampling is performed
on historical analogues alone. The reference baseline is the naive
climatological mean of the same training window; a positive CRPSS in
this setting therefore measures the additional skill that the
SPEI-tercile + Dirichlet--Multinomial weighting extracts beyond a
flat long-term average. A $+0.034$ skill score against a
moderately informative baseline is consistent with the published
range for tercile-class climatological forecasts in the tropics
\citep{Hersbach2000}; chasing a much larger CRPSS in this mode would
require leakage of target-cycle information, which would invalidate
the strict causality of the pipeline.

\paragraph{The framework offers two complementary value layers.}
The architecture exposes two operational outputs that target
different decision horizons. The cycle-level climatological
forecast (Section~\ref{sec:results_forecast}) supports
\emph{seasonal planning} decisions whose horizon is one to three
months ahead: equipment purchase, energy-supply contracting, water
allocation under reservoir constraints, and crop-financing risk
assessment. The rolling 5-day forecast
(Section~\ref{sec:results_rolling}) supports \emph{operational
day-to-day} decisions with median KGE $+0.32$ against the FAO-56
baseline, which is a reasonable working skill for a 5-day horizon
in tropical irrigation. These two layers cover a much larger share
of the producer's decision calendar than either layer alone, and the
framework is the deployment object that exposes both through the
same probabilistic interface (\verb|bwb forecast-sequential| and
\verb|bwb backtest-rolling|).

\paragraph{Calibration is the operational metric that matters most.}
On both layers the framework delivers credible intervals that are
either well-calibrated or conservatively over-covered: $0.954$
daily soil-water coverage on the climatological mode and $0.970$
prediction-interval coverage on the rolling mode, both at the
nominal $0.90$ level. For risk-aware irrigation in a
high-interannual-variability region like MATOPIBA, the asymmetric
cost of irrigation decisions favours a well-calibrated, slightly
conservative interval over a sharper but over-confident one: the
producer who treats a $90\%$ credible band as a $90\%$ band makes
risk-correct decisions; one who works with a nominal $90\%$ band
that is in reality a $60\%$ band systematically under-prepares for
deficit and over-spends on insurance against false alarms. A
calibrated forecast with modest sharpness frequently delivers more
operational value than an over-confident sharper one, and we made
that prioritisation an explicit design objective of the framework
(Section~\ref{sec:discussion_priors}).

\subsection{Climatological context of the validation window}

The validation window 2020/21--2024/25 spans a complete ENSO cycle
(Figure~\ref{fig:mei_timeseries}): La~Ni\~na conditions in
2020/21--2022/23 (the so-called ``triple-dip'' La~Ni\~na), strong
El~Ni\~no peaking during the 2023/24 cycle, and a return to neutral
conditions in 2024/25. The 2020--2022 ``triple-dip'' is one of the
ten multi-year La~Ni\~na events recorded since the 1920s and the
eighth such event since 1970, and the literature attributes its
recent acceleration to thermocline and zonal-advective feedbacks
under western-Pacific warming
\citep{Wang2023MultiyearLaNina,Geng2023ConsecutiveLaNina}; this
climatological framing is what allows the validation window to be
interpreted as a stress test of the framework under all three
canonical regimes rather than as a one-off of weather conditions. The CRPS by cycle
(Table~\ref{tab:forecast_by_cycle}) reflects this stratification:
the 2021/22 cycle, near the second peak of the ``triple-dip''
La~Ni\~na, exhibits the largest CRPS ($24.7$~mm), consistent with
the literature on heightened above-normal precipitation
intermittency during sustained Pacific cold events over the
Brazilian Cerrado \cite{Grimm2003,Marengo2018}. The 2023/24
El~Ni\~no and the 2022/23 transition cycles, by contrast, achieve
the lowest CRPS ($\approx 17$~mm) once the climatologically-informed
prior assigns the matching analogues a disproportionate weight. The
framework's ability to provide calibrated uncertainty under all
three regimes\,---\,La~Ni\~na, El~Ni\~no and neutral\,---\,supports
its deployment for operational decision support over the longer-term
horizon, and Figure~\ref{fig:mei_timeseries} situates the validation
window within the longer 1979--2025 record so that the reader can
appraise how representative the five tested cycles are of the ENSO
variability that the framework will encounter in deployment.

\subsection{Limitations and roadmap}\label{sec:discussion_limits}

Eight limitations are explicit. First, the validation is conducted
against a deterministic FAO-56 trajectory driven by the Xavier
gridded reanalysis; the Xavier dataset itself is a model output and
not in-situ soil moisture. External skill scores against measured
soil moisture from in-situ probe networks (e.g.~GLEAM~SMroot,
ESA~CCI~SM, or producer-installed capacitive sensors at the farm
level) are required before the framework can be claimed to
reproduce \emph{observed} soil-water content as opposed to the
modelled FAO-56 trajectory; this external validation is deferred
to a companion study. Second, the city-level fits are independent:
a hierarchical extension with partial pooling on $\theta_s$ across
hubs is methodologically defensible and would stabilise inference at
hubs with shorter records, but increases sampling cost; we consider
it a natural extension. Third, the sequential update of the
climatological forecast (Mode B) is at the \emph{cycle} level (one
update per growing season), not at the daily level, which can delay
adaptation to within-season regime shifts; the operational rolling
5-day forecast (Mode C) is also based on historical-analogue
resampling rather than on short-range numerical weather predictions,
so it does not currently exploit synoptic information from
operational NWP centres. Daily rolling forecasts integrating real
NWP fields (e.g.\ GEFS reforecast) are an operational extension that
we expose as a software hook
(\texttt{bwb forecast --members 31}) and demonstrate with a
synthetic ensemble in the documentation, while leaving real GEFS
integration to a companion paper.

Fourth, the validation window 2020/21--2024/25 spans a single
``triple-dip'' La~Ni\~na to El~Ni\~no transition; portability of the
calibration to other climate regimes (e.g.\ a sustained Atlantic
Multidecadal Oscillation positive phase, or to non-tropical
agricultural regions outside Brazil) requires a separate
out-of-region validation study. Fifth, the framework is currently
parameterised and tested for soybean on \textit{Latossolo}-class
soils representative of the MATOPIBA Cerrado; transfer to other
crop $\times$ soil combinations\,---\,for instance, more
clay-textured soils with higher AWC and slower drainage, or grain
crops with longer cycle lengths\,---\,requires re-elicitation of the
$K_c$ curve and the hydraulic prior centres, which the regional
configuration profile (\texttt{matopiba.toml}) is designed to make
straightforward but which has not been tested out-of-sample on
non-soybean grids. Sixth, no field A/B test against producer
practice has yet been conducted; the operational claim of the
framework is predicated on the FAO-56 baseline being a faithful
representation of the producer's reference irrigation rule, and an
end-to-end producer-in-the-loop deployment study is the appropriate
ultimate validation.

Seventh, the operational substitution of Xavier by the
NASA-POWER\,+\,Open-Meteo Archive fused product carries a residual
input error: the appendix INMET validation
(Table~\ref{tab:inmet-validation}) reports a pooled RMSE of
$0.54$\,mm\,d$^{-1}$ for ETo and $7.92$\,mm\,d$^{-1}$ for daily
precipitation against the in-situ stations, and the single-city A/B
probe at Balsas
suggests a non-trivial skill cost for the rolling forecast under
this substitution\,---\,a systematic ten-city assessment is
deferred to a companion deployment study. Eighth, all forecasts in
this work are issued at the \emph{municipality-centroid scale}: the
realised soil-water trajectory at a given farm within a hub may
deviate from the centroid forecast through within-property
spatial variability of soil texture, microclimate (slope,
aspect, riparian effects) and irrigation history. Spatial
downscaling to sub-municipality grids requires either an explicit
geostatistical layer over $\theta_s$ or a producer-supplied per-farm
override of the regional prior, both of which are exposed as
software hooks but not exercised in the validation reported here.

The combination of these eight items defines the roadmap for the
next research cycle: in-situ probe validation, hierarchical pooling
across hubs, real-NWP integration, multi-regime/cross-regional
portability tests, multi-crop transfer, producer A/B trials,
multi-city benchmarking of the fused operational product against
Xavier, and sub-municipality spatial downscaling.

A planned extension integrates a Bayesian-network imputation module
\cite{Ribeiro2023} for cases when one or more meteorological
variables are missing from the operational data feed\,---\,a
common scenario when station data are used in lieu of gridded
reanalysis. The Bayesian-network module will live in
\texttt{bwb.imputation.bn}, retain compatibility with the
sequential-forecast pipeline, and is queued as a future contribution.
The exploratory single-city probe of the fused-product substitution
(\verb|scripts/backtest_ab_fusion.py|) referenced in limitation 7
above is the prototype of the planned ten-city deployment study.

\subsection{Comparison with related work}

The most closely related published Bayesian water-balance framework
in Brazil is that of Ribeiro et al.~\cite{Ribeiro2023}, who used a
hierarchical Bayesian regression and a Bayesian network for
ETo estimation at a single station (Patroc\'inio/MG), achieving an
RMSE of $0.087$~mm~day$^{-1}$ relative to FAO-Penman--Monteith. The
present work extends that line in four directions:
(i)~the modelled state is the soil-water content, not just ETo;
(ii)~the validation grid spans 10 hubs and 5 cycles, against a
single-station validation in the prior work;
(iii)~the fitting horizon is 65 years (1961--2025) against 14 years
(2008--2022); and
(iv)~the framework is released as installable open-source software
with a command-line interface, a Docker container, and 97 unit and
integration tests, enabling deployment by domain practitioners
without re-implementation.

\paragraph{Consensus, open questions and speculation in the
surrounding literature.} For transparency we partition the
literature that bears on the present results into three categories
and locate each of our claims within them.

\textbf{What is consensus.} The recent literature is consistent
that the 2020--2022 ``triple-dip'' La~Ni\~na is one of a small
family of multi-year cold events whose frequency has accelerated
since 1970, with a documented mechanistic basis in
western-Pacific--warming feedbacks and in the precondition of a
preceding super- or central-Pacific El~Ni\~no
\citep{Wang2023MultiyearLaNina,Geng2023ConsecutiveLaNina}. The
hydrological literature is also consensual that probabilistic
forecasts in operational deployment must be \emph{calibrated} (PIT
uniform, coverage at the nominal level) and that proper scoring
rules such as CRPS are the appropriate scalar summaries
\citep{Hersbach2000,GneitingRaftery2007}. Our reported coverage
($0.92$ on the posterior-recovery test, $0.954$ on the
climatological forecast, $0.97$ on the rolling forecast) and our
reliance on CRPS as the operational scalar are aligned with this
consensus.

\textbf{What is still open.} Three questions remain debated. First,
whether the recent acceleration of multi-year La~Ni\~na events will
persist under continued warming and exactly how it interacts with
the Atlantic multidecadal oscillation is still under active study
\citep{Wang2023MultiyearLaNina}. Second, the operational substitute
for non-real-time gridded reanalyses (NASA POWER + ERA5 fused
products and similar) reproduces aggregate climatologies well but
under-estimates extremes of precipitation and over-estimates minimum
temperature in tropical sites. Third, the relative merits of
information-theoretic uncertainty objectives (e.g.\ RUMI of
\citealt{Pizarro2025RUMI}) versus the standard CRPS/coverage suite
for hydrological calibration remain an active research question.

\textbf{What is speculation, and what we do not claim.} We do not
claim that the framework produces the \emph{correct} field-scale
soil-moisture trajectory; the validation is consistent, in the sense
of \citet{Cook2006,Talts2018}, against the FAO-56 reference. The
extrapolation from this internal consistency to producer-level
financial decisions, to crops other than soybean and to soils other
than \textit{Latossolo}-class is not yet supported by the data
presented here, and is correctly characterised as the next research
cycle in Section~\ref{sec:discussion_limits}.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
% =====================================================================

We have presented \emph{bwb}, an open-source Python framework that
couples the FAO-56 daily water balance with a Bayesian probabilistic
inference engine in PyMC v5. The framework was validated across the
ten cropping hubs of the Brazilian MATOPIBA agricultural frontier and
five soybean growing seasons (2020/21--2024/25). A posterior-recovery
test on 150 city $\times$ soil-depth $\times$ cycle combinations
demonstrated faithful reproduction of the FAO-56 deterministic
process within calibrated uncertainty (median KGE $0.918$, coverage
$0.922$). A climatological sequential forecast on 50 city $\times$
cycle combinations\,---\,training strictly on years $< c$ and
forecasting cycle $c$ via Monte-Carlo resampling under a conjugate
Dirichlet--Multinomial posterior on SPEI tercile classes initialised
with a climatologically-informed prior on the 1961--2019 training
window\,---\,delivered a median CRPS of $18.2$~mm on seasonal
irrigation depth, a positive CRPSS of $+0.034$ against a naive
long-term-mean baseline, and a calibrated $0.954$ daily soil-water
coverage at the nominal $0.90$ level. A global Sobol' sensitivity analysis identified $\theta_s$ and
$\theta_{\mathrm{init}}$ as the dominant parameters, motivating
data-driven prior tuning. The framework is released under MIT license
with a command-line interface, Docker container and continuous
integration, and is architected to apply to any FAO-56 crop and any
region with daily $P$ and ETo time series.
